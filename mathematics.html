The geometry of one root triangle

The Lost
Triangle

One right triangle whose sides run in the ratio 1 : √2 : √3. Gregg Fleishman drew a lifetime of buildings, chairs, and playgrounds out of it. Click the tabs to see why it holds the whole system together.

01 — The proof

It lives inside the cube

Take any cube. Its edge is 1, its face diagonal is √2, its space diagonal is √3. Those three lengths are the triangle — it is simply the cube sliced corner to corner. The ratio isn't arbitrary; it's baked into the structure of 3D space.

02 — Two angles, one architecture

Two angles do all the work

The triangle's acute corners are α ≈ 35.25° and β ≈ 54.75° — exactly arctan(1/√2) and arctan √2 — and they add to a clean 90°. Every joint, fold, and corner in the work is some whole-number sum of just these two angles.

03 — The cubic family

A whole family from α + β

Tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, cuboctahedron, the truncations, the rhombicube — each one's dihedral angle is a tidy combination of α and β. The highlight on every solid splits its angle into those parts, so you can read the recipe straight off the geometry.

70.5° = 2α · 90° = α+β · 109.5° = 2β
125.25° = 2α+β · 144.75° = α+2β

04 — Infinitely buildable

Outward without end

Because the angles close perfectly, the solids stack with no gaps. The same triangle that fit inside one cube now fills all of space — the face-centred-cubic lattice, about 74% dense, repeating outward without end.
