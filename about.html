Background

Geometry
as a building system

Fleishman graduated from USC's School of Architecture in 1970 and spent nearly two decades working in commercial concrete construction. Through that period he was also researching a question: could the ratios embedded in the cube — the 1 : √2 : √3 triangle formed by its diagonals — become the basis of a complete building system?

By 1975 the first furniture emerged. The proportions of the plywood joints, the spans between nodes, the angles of intersection — all derive from the same geometry. The work is not decorative; it is structural logic made physical.

In the early 1990s, MoMA New York acquired pieces from his furniture collection. The work has since extended from chairs to domes, from domes to cathedral-scale temporary structures, from structures to the digital file systems that allow others to build from the same principles.

"The cube contains a triangle no one taught me about — a triangle with sides in exactly the ratio 1 : √2 : √3. Once you see it, you can't un-see it. Everything else followed from that."

— Gregg Fleishman
Milestones

Fifty years
of building

1970 Graduated USC School of Architecture. Began commercial concrete construction practice in Los Angeles.
1972 Started geometric research into cubic space-filling systems. Developed the "Lost Triangle" concept — the 1 : √2 : √3 ratio embedded in the cube's diagonals.
1975 First furniture designed using the geometric system. Birch plywood cut with CNC to exploit slot-and-pin joinery derived from the cubic geometry.
1990s MoMA New York acquires furniture from the collection. Work also entered the collections of Yale University and the Art Institute of Chicago.
2011–14 Burning Man. "Otic" series structures, Man Base Pistol, and the Temple of Whollyness — a six-story central space assembled from CNC-cut wood parts in 15 days, no metal fasteners.
2015–19 Wonderfruit Festival, Thailand. Solar Stage installations. Also Lucidity, Disclosure Fest, and Desert Hearts festivals in California.
2022– Sky Portal Series. Simplified slot-and-pin joinery. Panels intersect at 90°, drawing from cubic polyhedra. From single portals to infinite assemblies. Gallery opening at 201 S. Broadway, Los Angeles.
The underlying math

The geometry
made explorable

The geometry that drives Fleishman's work — the Lost Triangle, the rhombic dodecahedron, the space-filling system — is documented in an interactive web exhibit. It shows the mathematics from first principles: how the cube's diagonals create a triangle with sides in exact ratio 1 : √2 : √3, how that triangle "stands up" into space, and how the resulting cell packs the whole of space without gaps.
