Los Angeles · Since 1972

I have been researching how to build geometrically since 1972, and designing furniture since 1975. I am still at work in LA, a bit underground — it started with going to Burning Man, then a warehouse in Lincoln Heights, and now a gallery downtown.

What I learned about math, I learned from trying to build things more easily. It goes back to Pythagoras — or forward to Pythagoras, however you'd rather think of it. The angles and ratios hiding inside the cube became the joints, the spans, and the proportions of everything that followed.

Background

Geometry
as a building system

I came out of USC's School of Architecture in 1970 and spent the better part of two decades in commercial concrete construction. The whole time I was chasing one question: could the ratios sitting inside a cube — that 1 : √2 : √3 triangle the diagonals make — be enough to build a whole system from? Not as decoration. As the actual structural logic.

By 1975 the first furniture showed up. The plywood joints, the spans between nodes, the angles where things meet — all of it comes off the same geometry. A chair that's a puzzle to assemble: the first one took me thirty-odd prototypes and four years.

In the early nineties MoMA New York took pieces into the collection, along with Yale and the Art Institute of Chicago. After that I went further into the structural work — chairs to domes, domes to temples you can walk inside, and then to the digital files so other people can cut and build the same forms themselves.

"You know what a CUBE is — six equal squares at right angles forming a solid. Draw the diagonals inside it and you get a little triangle nobody taught me about, with sides one, square root of two, square root of three. What's the hypotenuse? You figure it out. Test in a week."

— Gregg Fleishman
Milestones

Fifty years
of building

1970 Graduated USC School of Architecture. Began commercial concrete construction practice in Los Angeles.
1972 Began the geometric research in earnest — the cubic, space-filling stuff. This is where the "Lost Triangle" turns up: the 1 : √2 : √3 ratio sitting in the cube's own diagonals, waiting.
1975 First furniture designed using the geometric system. Birch plywood cut with CNC to exploit slot-and-pin joinery derived from the cubic geometry.
1990s MoMA New York acquires furniture from the collection. Work also entered the collections of Yale University and the Art Institute of Chicago.
2011–14 Burning Man. The "Otic" structures, the Man Base, and the Temple of Whollyness — six stories, CNC-cut wood, raised in 15 days with not one metal fastener in it.
2015–19 Wonderfruit Festival, Thailand. Solar Stage installations. Also Lucidity, Disclosure Fest, and Desert Hearts festivals in California.
2022– Sky Portal Series. Simplified slot-and-pin joinery. Panels intersect at 90°, drawing from cubic polyhedra. From single portals to infinite assemblies. Gallery opening at 201 S. Broadway, Los Angeles.
The underlying math

The geometry
made explorable

The geometry that drives Fleishman's work — the Lost Triangle, the rhombic dodecahedron, the space-filling system — is documented in an interactive web exhibit. It shows the mathematics from first principles: how the cube's diagonals create a triangle with sides in exact ratio 1 : √2 : √3, how that triangle "stands up" into space, and how the resulting cell packs the whole of space without gaps.
