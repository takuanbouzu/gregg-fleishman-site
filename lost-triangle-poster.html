The geometry of one root triangle

The Lost
Triangle

From a single right triangle in the ratio 1 : √2 : √3 — the edge, face-diagonal and space-diagonal of the cube — an entire family of space-filling solids is derived. Two angles do all the work.

01 — The proof The triangle lives inside the cube
1² + (√2)² = 3 = (√3)²
edge 1 · face-diag √2 · space-diag √3

The right triangle with legs 1 and √2 closes on a hypotenuse of exactly √3 — the three characteristic lengths of the unit cube. Lay it from a corner across one face to the opposite corner and it sits inside the cube whole.

Its two acute angles, 35.25° and 54.75° — exactly arctan(1/√2) and arctan√2 — are the seed of everything that follows.

02 — Two angles, one architecture Every dihedral is a sum of two angles

Fleishman’s recent result: the two acute angles of the Lost Triangle — α = 35.25° and β = 54.75° — are the only angles needed to assemble the entire cubic family of polyhedra. Every dihedral angle in the table resolves into whole-number sums of α and β. Because these angles tile space without remainder, the 26-face rhombicube and its truncations stack outward through the face-centred-cubic lattice — a system that, in principle, is infinitely buildable.

03 — The cell that fills space Why this rhombus, and no other
flat face → solid cell
• close-packed spheres

Not just any rhombus. A square’s diagonals are equal; a generic rhombus’s are arbitrary. The silver rhombus alone carries the ratio 1 : √2 — the single proportion whose 70.5° / 109.5° angles (exactly 2α and 2β) let twelve identical faces close the rhombic dodecahedron.

That solid is precisely one close-packed sphere’s share of space — the region nearer to it than to any of its twelve neighbours — so stamping a single cell through the lattice fills all of space with no gaps.

04 — Infinitely buildable Close-packing of equal spheres

The reason the architecture never has to stop is the oldest packing problem in geometry. Equal spheres pack most densely in the face-centred-cubic lattice — about 74% of space filled, the maximum any arrangement can reach (Kepler’s conjecture, proven by Hales). Each sphere touches exactly twelve others, and the octahedral gap between them sits at a distance of √2 — the face-diagonal of the Lost Triangle.

Join every pair of touching centres and space resolves into a honeycomb of regular tetrahedra and octahedra whose dihedral angles are precisely 70.5° and 109.5° — 2α and 2β. Its dual is the rhombic-dodecahedral honeycomb: Gregg’s stacking, repeating outward without end.
